Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"A shared cardiac–skeletal-muscle ageing transcriptional signature in heart failure implicates a miR-15/16–CCND1 regulatory axis"** for consideration as a Research Article in *Genes & Genomics*.

Skeletal-muscle wasting is a common and prognostically important comorbidity of heart failure (HF), yet whether the failing heart and ageing skeletal muscle share a definable molecular programme — and whether any such overlap is simply the chronological ageing common to both patient groups — has not been tested directly. In this study we integrate publicly available transcriptomes of end-stage failing left-ventricular myocardium and ageing skeletal muscle and, importantly, subject the resulting shared signature to a battery of robustness analyses designed to pre-empt the usual confounders of cross-tissue bioinformatic studies.

Our principal findings are: (1) the two tissues share a statistically significant set of dysregulated genes (hypergeometric p = 1.0 × 10⁻³) enriched for extracellular-matrix, NAD/immune-metabolic and mTOR programmes; (2) a directional signature score discriminates HF in an independent cohort (AUC 0.92) and ageing muscle across five independent cohorts (random-effects pooled AUC 0.76, I² = 0%); (3) critically, the HF component of the signature is **independent of patient age** (AUC 0.95 after age adjustment; p = 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁸) and of HF etiology, whereas the muscle component remains ageing-linked — a distinction we make explicit rather than gloss over; and (4) candidate upstream regulation converges on the canonical miR-15/16/195/497 → CCND1 cell-cycle axis, with single-cell mapping (1.9 million cells) placing the NAD/immune hub NAMPT in myeloid cells and the matrix hub FBLN1 in stromal cells of both tissues.

We have deliberately framed this work as a **hypothesis-generating, fully reproducible map of a candidate cardio-muscle ageing axis rather than a validated diagnostic biomarker**, and we report — rather than hide — the limitations a careful reader would probe (the muscle data represent ageing muscle rather than clinically phenotyped sarcopenia; the HF cohort is end-stage HFrEF; the CLIP-supported network is dominated by CCND1 and is not enriched beyond 3′UTR length at the panel level). We believe this transparent, robustness-first treatment of an under-studied muscle–heart axis is well suited to the readership of *Genes & Genomics*.
